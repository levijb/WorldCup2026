## Today's Matches

**Australia vs. Egypt — 2:00 PM ET | AT&T Stadium, Arlington, Texas**
Mohamed Salah's hamstring is the single biggest variable on tonight's slate — scans confirmed a strain after he limped off against Iran, and Egypt's own federation is calling his availability a genuine game-time question rather than a lock either way. Egypt arrived here unbeaten and made history doing it, a draw with Belgium and a 1-1 with Iran (a stoppage-time VAR-disallowed equalizer cost them top spot) sandwiching a 3-1 win over New Zealand, while Australia finished second in Group D on a single 2-0 win over Turkey and have failed to score in back-to-back matches since. Beyond Salah, left-back Ahmed Fattouh (hamstring tear) is considered highly unlikely to feature and Mohamed Abdelmonem (ankle) is being managed hour to hour, while Australia is down to the bare cupboard out wide with Jacob Italiano (groin) and Mathew Leckie (hamstring) both ruled out for the rest of the tournament. AT&T Stadium's retractable roof takes Texas heat off the table entirely — this plays as a neutral, climate-controlled match regardless of the 2 PM kickoff. Egypt's five different group-stage scorers suggest the attack survives without Salah better than the panic implies, but Australia's defense — allowing less expected goals per shot faced than anyone left in the bracket outside Spain — makes this a genuine coin-flip that could easily go the distance.

**Argentina vs. Cape Verde — 6:00 PM ET | Hard Rock Stadium, Miami Gardens, Florida**
This is Round of 32's most lopsided pairing on paper, but the real story isn't Argentina's class so much as Lionel Messi's absurd finishing — six goals from just 2.37 expected goals through the group stage, the largest overperformance of any frontline scorer left in the bracket. Argentina finished a perfect 3-0-0 in Group J (3-0 Algeria, 2-0 Austria, 3-1 Jordan) and have conceded a single goal all tournament, while Cape Verde's three draws — against Spain, Uruguay, and Saudi Arabia — made them the smallest nation by population ever to reach a men's World Cup knockout round, built around 40-something goalkeeper Vozinha and a back line that sits as deep as any team left in the field. Cape Verde reports no fresh fitness concerns beyond winger Telmo Arcanjo (hamstring, already ruled out), while Argentina gets Cristian Romero back as a game-time call after a minor knock, with Nicolas Otamendi ready to deputize if needed. Hard Rock Stadium's evening humidity is a mild factor at a 6 PM kickoff but nothing close to the afternoon heat-index matches elsewhere on the slate. Argentina's question here isn't whether they win — it's whether Cape Verde's low block, which has already frustrated a rotated Spain and a well-organized Uruguay side, can hold out against a team with considerably more individual quality than either of those opponents.

**Colombia vs. Ghana — 9:30 PM ET | Arrowhead Stadium, Kansas City, Missouri**
Ghana have managed exactly one shot on target in each of their last two matches, and their most individually talented attacker, Antoine Semenyo, still hasn't registered a shot on target at this entire tournament — three attempts, 0.13 expected goals — which says more about tonight's likely goal count than either team's group-stage record does. Colombia topped Group K unbeaten and never conceded more than once (3-1 over Uzbekistan, 1-0 over Congo DR, 0-0 with Portugal), while Ghana scraped through as a third-place qualifier after beating Panama on a 95th-minute winner, drawing England, and losing to Croatia — a group stage that produced all of two goals. Semenyo is carrying an ankle knock from the Croatia match and is expected to start regardless, a goalkeeper issue for Lawrence Ati-Zigi hands the gloves to Benjamin Asare, and Colombia reports a clean bill of health with Luis Suarez fully available after a minor doubt earlier in the group stage. Arrowhead at a 9:30 PM kickoff is standard sea-level conditions — nothing structural to factor in. Colombia have won every match this tournament by exactly one goal, which points toward another tight scoreline, but a defense that's kept two straight clean sheets against an attack this blunt is where the real edge in this match lives.

<a name="predictions"></a>
## Predictions

#### Australia vs. Egypt
Egypt's group-stage form says more than the record alone: they scored in every match, used five different goalscorers, and did it while managing injuries and rotation at various points — this isn't a team that collapses around one absence, even Salah's. Australia's defensive shape is the best story of their tournament, built on a settled back three that's faced fewer clean scoring chances than almost anyone left in the draw, but the flip side is an attack that's gone two straight matches without finding the net and has scored just twice all tournament. Omar Marmoush becomes the unambiguous first read for Egypt regardless of how much of this match Salah plays, and Australia's left side, where 35-year-old Aziz Behich lines up, is the most direct route for Egypt's pace if Salah is even available for a substitute cameo. Neither side has the attacking profile to be favored to score more than once.

This has the shape of a match decided by a single moment rather than sustained pressure from either side — Egypt's attacking talent against Australia's defensive discipline is close enough that extra time is a live outcome. If Salah is unavailable or heavily limited, Australia's counter through Nestory Irankunda gets meaningfully more dangerous, since Egypt's patched-together back line (Fattouh out, Abdelmonem uncertain) has to defend in unfamiliar combinations. Predicted score: 1–1 (Draw — goes to ET/PKs, Egypt advances).

#### Argentina vs. Cape Verde
Argentina's group-stage numbers are built on patience finding a crack rather than overwhelming volume — three wins, one goal conceded, and a Messi who needs fewer clean touches than almost any forward alive to convert a chance. Cape Verde's approach hasn't changed all tournament: a low, compact block that cedes the ball by design, daring teams to solve them in tight spaces rather than in transition, and it's worked against Spain and Uruguay, teams with more individual quality in most other spots on the pitch than Cape Verde has anywhere. The swing factor is Vozinha, whose shot-stopping has been the difference between Cape Verde's three draws and three defeats, and whether he can produce a similar level of goalkeeping against Messi specifically — a different category of finisher than anyone Cape Verde has faced.

For Cape Verde to survive regulation, they need Vozinha at his best plus a moment of real chaos — a set piece, a turnover, something outside the run of play — because they haven't shown the attacking tools to create sustained danger against a defense this well-organized. Argentina's patience should eventually find the breakthrough without needing to fully open the game up. Predicted score: 2–0 (Argentina).

#### Colombia vs. Ghana
Colombia's tournament has been defined by control rather than dominance — they've won each match by a single goal while limiting opponents to almost nothing, and that profile matches up cleanly against a Ghana side that has scored only twice in three group matches and leans on Thomas Partey to disrupt rather than any genuine attacking outlet to create. Ghana's best moments have come from set pieces and chaos rather than sustained buildup, which is precisely the kind of game Colombia's defense — Sanchez, Lucumi, and a midfield screen in front of them — has been built to deny all tournament. Semenyo's underlying numbers, zero shots on target through three matches, suggest Ghana's attacking hope runs through Jordan Ayew and moments of individual improvisation more than any structured plan.

If Ghana can nick an early goal or force a set-piece moment, their low block becomes a genuinely difficult puzzle for 80-plus minutes — but nothing in their group-stage shot data suggests that's the likely path. Colombia's control and James Rodriguez's range of passing should be enough to find the decisive moments without needing to fully commit numbers forward. Predicted score: 2–0 (Colombia).

## Around the Tournament

- Seven nations are into their first-ever World Cup knockout round this tournament — Cape Verde, Egypt, Ivory Coast, South Africa, Congo DR, Canada, and Bosnia and Herzegovina — though half that group is already out, eliminated in the very round they'd never previously reached.
- Cape Verde's Vozinha remains the tournament's breakout story: saves against Spain and Saudi Arabia helped the smallest nation by population ever to reach a men's World Cup knockout round, and his social following has grown roughly thirtyfold since the group stage began.
- Messi's goal against Jordan extended his own record to seven consecutive World Cup games with a goal scored, pushing his career total to 19 — a strike tonight against Cape Verde would make it eight straight.
- Switzerland's win over Algeria was their first World Cup knockout victory in more than seven decades, with Manuel Akanji central to a defense that's now conceded the fewest goals of any team left in the bracket.
- The Golden Boot race is a two-way duel for now: Messi and Kylian Mbappé sit tied at six goals apiece, with Mbappé's brace against Sweden also making him the all-time leading scorer in men's World Cup knockout-stage history.

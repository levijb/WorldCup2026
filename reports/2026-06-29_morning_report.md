## Todays Matches

**Brazil vs. Japan — 1:00 PM ET | NRG Stadium, Houston | Round of 32**
The most tactically interesting match of the day, and the one where the favorite deserves the most scrutiny. Brazil's 3-0 wins over Haiti and Scotland came against teams that didn't qualify as top-eight third-place sides — Japan is a different test entirely. The Samurai Blue went unbeaten through Group F (2-2 Netherlands, 4-0 Tunisia, 1-1 Sweden), conceding just three goals, and beat Brazil 3-2 in a friendly last October. Raphinha is out with a hamstring injury suffered against Haiti, removing Brazil's primary corner and set-piece taker and their most creative wide outlet — Rayan or Luiz Henrique will deputize but it's a significant quality drop. Japan's 3-4-2-1 with Ko Itakura doubtful after a first-half injury vs. Sweden means their back three may be reshuffled: Taniguchi likely slots in alongside Hiroki Ito and Seko. Brazil's structural problem is their makeshift right back — Danilo at 34 — who will face Japan's Keito Nakamura bombing forward all afternoon. NRG Stadium in Houston is a dome venue, heat neutralized; no weather variable. Brazil are the better squad, Vinicius Jr. is the best individual on the pitch, but this is not a 3-0.

**Germany vs. Paraguay — 4:30 PM ET | Gillette Stadium, Foxborough | Round of 32**
Germany are massive favorites and deserve to be, but their path through Paraguay is not as straightforward as -340 implies. Julian Nagelsmann has a well-documented selection problem: Deniz Undav — 3 goals, 2 assists off the bench in the group stage, the most productive German per minute — keeps getting benched for Kai Havertz, who has gone scoreless in two of three group games after his Curaçao brace. Paraguay's tactical identity is the issue for Germany: Gustavo Alfaro's 5-4-1 low block held out Turkey 1-0 and ground a 0-0 with Australia after conceding 4 to the USA in their opener. Diego Gómez is suspended. Omar Alderete is doubtful with a knee injury. But Miguel Almirón returns from suspension and gives Paraguay something on the counter. Nathaniel Brown is managing a muscle issue for Germany. Nico Schlotterbeck is out for the tournament. Foxborough is sea-level, outdoor — weather is a mild variable but nothing material. Germany win this, but the structure of the game will be tighter than the odds imply.

**Netherlands vs. Morocco — 9:00 PM ET | Estadio BBVA, Monterrey, Mexico | Round of 32**
This is the match of the day and the most contested on the board. Both teams went unbeaten in the group stage — the Netherlands won Group F with 7pts (2-2 Japan, 5-1 Sweden, 3-1 Tunisia), Morocco went unbeaten in Group C with 7pts (1-1 Brazil, 1-0 Scotland, 4-2 Haiti). Neither conceded more than twice in any group match. Morocco without Nayef Aguerd (groin, pre-tournament) and Abde Ezzalzouli (knee) are thinner in defense and wide, but Ismael Saibari has scored in all three group matches and Brahim Díaz creates from the ten. The Netherlands have genuine injury fragility: Timber (groin), Simons (ACL), and De Ligt (back) are all missing — the defensive and creative losses are meaningful. Frenkie de Jong has been excellent, averaging 5+ recoveries per match, and Brian Brobbey has added 3 goals. This game is in Monterrey at altitude (~1,770 feet — moderate but real at 90 minutes). Morocco won 13 straight competitive matches inside 90 minutes before the Brazil draw this tournament. The +270 Morocco ML at FanDuel is not a novelty bet.

## Predictions

#### Brazil vs. Japan

Brazil's structural problem against Japan is real: without Raphinha, their right side loses pace and crosses, and Danilo at right back is 34 years old against Nakamura's energy. Japan's 3-4-2-1 with wide wing-backs designed to exploit exactly that space has worked against Netherlands and Sweden in this tournament — they drew the Dutch when the Dutch had Simons and didn't. Vinicius on the left is Brazil's most dangerous weapon and he will get his moments against whoever Japan put at right wing-back. Casemiro and Guimarães control the midfield pivot, but Japan's compact 3-4-2-1 denies space in the center — Brazil will need their wide play to generate. Neymar is on the bench and provides a genuine late-game option; the question is whether Ancelotti uses him before the 60th minute.

Brazil find a goal through Vinicius in the first half, and Japan's discipline — unbeaten in 10 — keeps them in it. Japan equalize through a set piece or Doan counterattack in the second half. Brazil do not lose this, but Japan have the organization to make it go to extra time if they get the equalizer. **Predicted score: 2–1 (Brazil) — but Japan cover the spread and the game goes deep into the 80s before Brazil seal it.**

#### Germany vs. Paraguay

Paraguay's 5-4-1 low block is the most organized defensive structure Germany have faced this tournament — more disciplined than Ecuador, who scored on a Germany mistake rather than dominant play. Musiala and Wirtz have been "neat and tidy" in Nagelsmann's phrasing but haven't blown anyone away; Havertz is 0 goals in 2 of 3 group games. Paraguay's plan is clear: sit at 5-4-1, be physical in midfield, allow Germany wide, and counter through Almirón and Enciso. Germany's forward line scores a lot when they have space — Curaçao — and scores less when they don't — Ecuador gave up 0.65 xG. That said, the quality gap between Germany and Paraguay is real. Germany will eventually find the gaps, especially once Undav enters, and Paraguay lack the upper-90 quality to convert their counter chances consistently.

Germany get the first goal before the hour, Undav enters and adds a second in the final 20 minutes, Paraguay steal a consolation. The margin lands around 2. **Predicted score: 2–1 (Germany)**

#### Netherlands vs. Morocco

Morocco's 32-game unbeaten streak heading into the 2022 World Cup semifinal run was not the ceiling — they've won or drawn 32 straight inside 90 minutes in the competitive run since. Saibari has scored in all three group games and is Morocco's most explosive forward movement. The Netherlands have more raw talent in Brobbey, Gakpo, and a fit Frenkie de Jong — but the Timber/Simons/De Ligt absences are not cosmetic. Their back line has been tested more than the scoreline of 3-1 vs. Tunisia suggests — they conceded to every team they played. This game in Monterrey at altitude means a slower second half, which favors Morocco's defensive shape. The under lean is real at altitude; both teams will be conservative through 60 minutes.

Morocco defend well, allow the Dutch possession without dangerous penetration, and find one goal through a Saibari run or a Brahim Díaz moment. Netherlands press for an equalizer and create chances but Morocco's 4-2-3-1 absorbs pressure at this altitude. **Predicted score: 1–1 after 90 (Morocco advance via extra time or penalties).** Even in a regulation prediction, this goes the distance.

## Around the Tournament

- The Norway "Viking Row" has become the unofficial cultural moment of this World Cup. Norwegian fans perform a synchronized rowing motion in stadiums and streets — on escalators in Boston's South Station, on New York City subway trains, in Times Square, and reportedly inside Norway's parliament back home. The players joined in after their Senegal win, with Martin Ødegaard beating a drum while Haaland and the squad rowed in unison. It is the logical descendant of Iceland's 2016 "Viking Clap," but louder and more physically absurd.

- DR Congo superfan Michel "Lumumba Vea" Mboladinga stood completely motionless for the entire duration of their group stage match against Colombia, dressed as assassinated Prime Minister Patrice Lumumba — in honor of the leader who brought the country to independence in 1960. He held the same pose he first performed at last year's Africa Cup of Nations. DR Congo won, and he goes to Atlanta now.

- Stephen Eustaquio, managing a muscle injury and substituted off early in the Switzerland loss, scored the 95th-minute winner yesterday to send Canada to the Round of 16. His goal came off an Alistair Johnston clearance that dropped directly to him outside the box — he struck it purely and immediately began celebrating before it crossed the line. The celebration from a player who'd spent 80 minutes managing his own fitness will be one of the tournament's enduring images.

- Carlo Ancelotti is the first foreign coach to manage Brazil at a World Cup in the country's history. He is also 66 years old, has already won the Champions League five times, and was apparently persuaded back into international management after walking away from Real Madrid. If Brazil win this tournament, that sentence becomes one of sport's stranger facts.

- Ralf Rangnick's post-Algeria postgame quote on the 3-3 draw — "If Alfred Hitchcock had written such a drama, I would have said he was completely mad" — has made the rounds. It's the truest thing any coach has said at this World Cup. Algeria led 3-2 in the 90+3rd minute and were eliminated. Kalajdzic headed in the equalizer in the 90+6th. Both teams advanced. Rangnick was smiling when he said it.

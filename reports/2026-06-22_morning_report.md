## TODAY'S MATCHES

**Argentina vs. Austria — 1:00 PM ET | AT&T Stadium, Dallas | Group J**
The effective Group J final, with both sides sitting on three points and the winner almost certainly advancing with a match to spare. Argentina dismantled Algeria 3–0 on Messi's first career World Cup hat-trick; Austria edged Jordan 3–1 in a tighter performance than the scoreline suggested, relying on an own goal and a stoppage-time penalty to make it look comfortable. The underlying gap is real — Argentina held Algeria to zero shots on target, while Austria's defensive structure faces a categorically different examination against Scaloni's counter-pressing setup. AT&T Stadium's retractable roof and climate control remove the Dallas heat as a variable, eliminating one reason to expect second-half drop-off. Messi enters one goal from Klose's all-time World Cup scoring record and Argentina make him the confirmed penalty taker — the motivation is structural, not ceremonial. The bet is Argentina to win and, on the totals, the over is worth examining given both sides scored three apiece in their openers.

**France vs. Iraq — 5:00 PM ET | Lincoln Financial Field, Philadelphia | Group I**
France need a win to effectively seal Group I qualification and are priced accordingly. They were below their best against Senegal and still scored three times in the final 30 minutes — the attack has another gear it hasn't needed to use yet. Iraq's 4–1 loss to Norway exposed a midfield that gets pulled wide by pace and runs out of defensive shape late in matches; they conceded twice in the final 25 minutes. Mbappé carries 14 World Cup goals into this match and is hunting the Golden Boot; Olise and Barcola provide depth that Iraq cannot match across 90 minutes. Lincoln Financial Field is an open-air venue with temperatures around 80°F expected — the IFAB fatigue factor favors France's fresher legs off the bench. The spread is the angle: France -2.5 is available around -118 to -135 depending on book, and Iraq's defensive profile against pressure-intensive sides makes a large margin the most probable outcome.

**Norway vs. Senegal — 8:00 PM ET | MetLife Stadium, East Rutherford | Group I**
Norway come in on three points and unbeaten in 16 of their last 17 matches; Senegal come in on zero and knowing a loss likely ends their tournament. The tactical tension is real: Senegal are not Iraq, and Koulibaly's defensive command gives them a physical platform to slow Haaland's service. Senegal generated only 0.53 xG against France despite 47% possession — their finishing is the soft underbelly of an otherwise organized side. Norway's sweeping transitions and Haaland's hold-up play feed off that kind of pressure, but a desperate Senegal side will sit deeper here than they could against France. MetLife in early evening should be cooler than the afternoon venues — the Norway physical style suits it. The draw is legitimately priced and the sharp community has been noting that Senegal's structural discipline makes this closer than a first-place/third-place points differential suggests. Haaland's shots volume is the prop angle.

**Jordan vs. Algeria — 11:00 PM ET | Levi's Stadium, Santa Clara | Group J**
Both sides sit on zero points after being beaten by Austria and Argentina respectively. Algeria lost to Messi's hat-trick in Kansas City; Jordan lost 3–1 to Austria. A win keeps either alive for a third-place berth; a draw may not be enough for either. Algeria are the higher-ranked side and carried more of the attacking play against Argentina than the 3–0 scoreline implies. Jordan offered resistance against Austria but their attack never generated enough to suggest they can win this if Algeria show up organized. Levi's Stadium is a reasonable altitude-neutral venue at sea level. Market will have this priced closer to even, and without live odds there is no strong directional edge to publish — treat this as an information-only match unless the opening line offers clear value on Algeria.

## AROUND THE TOURNAMENT

Norway's supporters have turned the MetLife surroundings into a series of coordinated Viking longship rowing routines on every escalator within a mile of the stadium. It started in Boston after the Iraq win and has since spread to every venue Norway travels to. At this point the synchronised rowing is more reliable than any tactical insight into how Senegal plan to stop Haaland.

Deniz Undav has now scored a brace off the bench in back-to-back World Cup matches for Germany, including a stoppage-time winner to beat Ivory Coast. He has four minutes of first-half football at this tournament and seven goals in the context of the conversation about who Germany's starting striker should be. Ralf Rangnick is, apparently, fine with this situation.

Cape Verde — a country of 570,000 people making their World Cup debut — have two points from their first two matches against Spain and Uruguay. Their goalkeeper Vozinha has become the tournament's most talked-about figure outside of Messi and Mbappé. Their fans in the stadium have been filmed celebrating every neutral event in the match as if it were a goal, which, at this point, seems reasonable.

Japan's 4–0 win over Tunisia was confirmed as the largest winning margin for an Asian nation in World Cup history, and the 1,000th match overall in the tournament's history. The Japanese fans stayed behind afterward to clean the stadium. Naturally.

The Belgian red card against Iran has reignited the debate about the new conduct red-card rule for dissent and time-wasting — the sending-off appeared to come in the 56th minute with the scores at 0–0, confirming that teams trailing and drawing are equally at risk when a referee applies the rule strictly.

**Spain vs. Saudi Arabia — 12:00 PM ET | Mercedes-Benz Stadium, Atlanta | Group H**
Spain open their tournament at altitude-adjacent Atlanta against a Saudi side that drew Cape Verde — yes, that actually happened, or something close to it based on early tournament color. The line has drifted slightly toward Spain from -1100 to -1000, which is a mild sharp signal, while Saudi Arabia shortened from +2200 to +2200, suggesting some money on the upset hedge. The betting angle here is Spain win but the total — the under has structural support given the conditions and Spain's tendency to control rather than pile on.

**Belgium vs. Iran — 3:00 PM ET | SoFi Stadium, Inglewood, CA | Group G**
Belgium are heavy favorites at -235, which implies roughly 70% true probability — that feels about right for a side with De Bruyne, Lukaku, and a functional if aging spine. Iran are compact and disciplined defensively, and they'll look to frustrate. The expanded VAR scope is worth flagging: Belgium's physicality means they'll draw contact in the box, and Iran's defensive structure invites set-piece situations. SoFi is a neutral-conditions, sea-level venue — no environmental edge either way. Belgium to win is the obvious play; the question is whether the price is worth taking at -235, which is thin.

**Uruguay vs. Cape Verde — 6:00 PM ET | Hard Rock Stadium, Miami | Group H**
Hard Rock in Miami in June afternoon heat — this is a hostile environment for any side relying on high-tempo football. Cape Verde are the story of the early tournament after their draw against Spain; they play compact, direct football and are dangerous on the counter. Uruguay are a known quantity: defensively organized, aggressive in midfield, with Darwin Núñez as the primary outlet up front. The Cape Verde draw line has shortened from +340 to +320, suggesting some money on the draw — Cape Verde held Spain to a draw, and Uruguay at -220 feels like the market respects the Cape Verde threat. The heat at Hard Rock works against a high press; expect a tight, physical match with fewer goals than the odds might imply.

**New Zealand vs. Egypt — 9:00 PM ET | BC Place, Vancouver | Group G**
Egypt are short favorites at -170, which implies 63% probability — steep for a side whose attacking output has been modest historically in World Cup football. New Zealand have drifted further to +500 from +450, which is the market pricing in their limited ceiling. BC Place is a closed dome — conditions are controlled, which removes the weather wildcard that helps smaller sides sometimes steal results. Egypt will look to Mohammed Salah to create and convert; New Zealand will defend and look for transitions. The draw at +300 shortened from +320, which is the one marginal sharp signal here.

## AROUND THE TOURNAMENT

- Netherlands scoring five goals against Sweden — while missing Timber, Simons, and De Ligt — is one of the more confusing results of the early tournament. The pre-tournament fade thesis still has structural merit, but Van Dijk and Gakpo have apparently not received the memo. Odds compilers are already adjusting; the +1800 tournament price is gone.

- The Cape Verde goalkeeper Vozinha story is worth sitting with: 40 years old, seven saves against Spain in his nation's first World Cup appearance, in tears on the pitch because his mother couldn't get a visa to watch him. If you need a reason to care about football beyond the betting angles, there it is.

- Messi's hat trick against Algeria is generating the expected volume of hyperbole, but the image underneath is worth noting — Algeria gave him space in the middle of the field, repeatedly, and he punished them each time. The defending champion fade thesis applies structurally, but you'd want Argentina to beat someone with a functioning defensive shape before pricing them down significantly.

- The attendance figures debate — 63,000 average across the first eight matches with some notable empty-seat footage circulating — is one of those arguments that will never be resolved to anyone's satisfaction because the official numbers and the television images are telling different stories. FIFA will cite the first; everyone else will cite the second.

- Germany recalling 40-year-old Manuel Neuer from retirement and then watching him perform adequately against Ivory Coast is either the most German thing to happen in this tournament or the least surprising given their goalkeeper situation. Ter Stegen's absence is a genuine issue; Neuer's presence is a calculated gamble that is, so far, not visibly backfiring.

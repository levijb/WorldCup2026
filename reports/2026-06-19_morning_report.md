## AROUND THE TOURNAMENT

- Vozinha, the 40-year-old Cape Verde goalkeeper, made seven saves in a goalless draw against Spain and broke down in tears on the pitch when the final whistle blew. His mother couldn't get a US visa in time to attend. Seven saves. Against Spain. At 40. The World Cup occasionally remembers to be remarkable.

- The Iran vs. New Zealand atmosphere in Los Angeles was reportedly unlike anything else at the tournament — sections of Iranian supporters holding pre-revolutionary lion-and-sun flags in open protest, with some supporters audibly booing their own team. FIFA's flag restrictions, it turns out, are somewhat aspirational. Football as proxy war has a long history; this was a particularly charged installment.

- VAR made its tournament statement during France vs. Senegal at MetLife, when referee Alireza Faghani reviewed a Mbappé appeal — watched the monitor, and waved it off. Whether that was the correct call is genuinely unclear. What is clear is that "VAR reviewed and confirmed" does not appear to be producing the certainty it promised. The debate about whether expanded VAR scope produces more justice or simply more controversy remains very much open.

- Curaçao, population 150,000, land area roughly the size of a mid-sized American city's suburb, scored in their first-ever World Cup match — the smallest nation to do so in the men's game. Livano Comenencia's 21st-minute goal will be on highlight reels in Willemstad for a generation.

- Mexico's Azteca moment is worth dwelling on. Raúl Jiménez had never scored on the World Cup stage before Thursday. He headed home against South Korea with 80,000 in the stadium that has hosted more World Cup football than any other ground on earth. That it came at altitude, at home, in a tournament Mexico is co-hosting, and at a ground with that history — the occasion delivered exactly what it was supposed to.

## TODAY'S MATCHES

**USA vs. Australia — 3:00 PM ET | Lumen Field, Seattle | Group D**
The United States' home tournament begins in earnest here, and the crowd at Lumen Field will make this feel like a playoff game whether it deserves to or not. Pulisic is reportedly fit to start after missing time with a leg knock — his availability is the difference between a functional USA attack and a toothless one. Australia come in with questions of their own after Mohamed Toure picked up a calf issue, though he is also expected to feature. The bookmakers have USA at -165, which bakes in significant home-atmosphere premium; against an Australian side that have been quietly capable in qualifying, that price requires scrutiny. The draw at +330 is the line that deserves a second look.

**Scotland vs. Morocco — 6:00 PM ET | Gillette Stadium, Boston | Group C**
Scotland — fresh off their first World Cup victory in 36 years, however that was managed — now face a significantly sterner test in Morocco, who enter as the group's presumptive second-place finisher. Morocco's goalkeeper Bounou is doubtful with an arm injury and defender Aguerd is carrying a knock, which creates real structural uncertainty at the back. Scotland will set up deep and compact, as Clarke has never once in his managerial career deviated from this approach, and with Morocco potentially weakened in defence the tactical picture is more balanced than Morocco -135 implies. This is a match that wants to be a draw.

**Brazil vs. Haiti — 8:30 PM ET | Lincoln Financial Field, Philadelphia | Group C**
Brazil's tournament debut, and it arrives already carrying injury news. Neymar is confirmed out, and reporting indicates Estêvão and Rodrygo are also unavailable — three of their most dangerous attacking options gone before they kick a ball. Haiti will be organised and low, offering space reluctantly and probably parking entirely. Brazil are -900, which implies near-certainty, and the match total is likely to be set around 3.5 — the question is whether a depleted attacking unit can actually produce the volume needed against a disciplined defensive block. Against Haiti, probably yes, but the price reflects a Brazil squad that was intact.

**Turkey vs. Paraguay — 11:00 PM ET | Levi's Stadium, Santa Clara | Group D**
The late kick in Santa Clara is genuinely difficult to read, which in itself is useful information. Turkey enter as mild favourites at +105, Paraguay at +285, with the draw a very live +245. The Czech Republic vs. Mexico line movement yesterday — Czechia shortening dramatically from +370 to +210 — suggests Group A dynamics are shifting in ways that may not be unrelated to how Group D shakes out in terms of third-place qualification pressure. Both teams need points; neither has the luxury of conservative football. This has the profile of an open, slightly chaotic match where the total goes over and a winner is decided by an individual moment.

## AROUND THE TOURNAMENT

- Messi, at 38 years old, scored a hat-trick against Algeria to equal Miroslav Klose's all-time World Cup scoring record of 16 goals. He reportedly wept on the pitch at the final whistle. The man is conducting his own farewell tour and the rest of the tournament is merely setting and lighting.

- Curaçao — population 150,000, land area 171 square miles — have scored in a World Cup. Livano Comenencia's goal makes them the smallest nation ever to do so at the men's tournament. One imagines there are roughly 150,000 people in Willemstad who will not be sleeping tonight.

- The Tartan Army, fresh off Scotland's first World Cup win in 36 years, apparently decided to mark the occasion by attending a Boston Red Sox game at Fenway Park in full kilt regalia. This is either the most Scottish thing to happen at a World Cup or a deeply rational use of a free afternoon — possibly both.

- Referee Tori Penso made history as only the second woman to officiate a men's World Cup match, awarding a late penalty in Czechia vs. South Africa via VAR review. The expanded VAR scope for 2026 is already generating exactly the controversy you would expect, with mandatory water breaks in air-conditioned, roofed stadiums adding a layer of procedural absurdity that has united fans of opposing nations in irritation — which is, at minimum, a form of unity.

- Jordan scored their first-ever World Cup goal: Ali Iyad Olwan won the ball in midfield, drove 50 yards, and curled a finish into the far post. For a country experiencing their debut tournament, that is an extraordinarily good way to open an account.
